What is 3D Printing?

3D Printing (Additive Manufacturing) is a suite of technologies for turning digital models into real, physical, three-dimensional objects.

  • In a design environment such as AutoCAD, you can build virtual objects. You can then convert these virtual objects into 3D model files suitable for 3D printing.
  • Using a 3D scanner, you can digitize objects, generating 3d digital scans. You can then assemble these scans into 3D models suitable for printing.
  • Using photogrammetry, you can take multiple overlapping photographs of a physical object from several angles. Then, through software that infers distances and spatial relationships from object photographs and camera data, you can stitch these photos of the physical object into a 3D model suitable for printing.

Applications

Sustainable Consumption: Spare Parts

“One of the promising functionalities of 3D printing is the possibility to produce spare parts. As a result, it should become much easier to fix, instead of replace, a malfunctioning device as the missing part will just come rolling out of the printer. This might result in a more engaging and sustainable relationship with mass-produced artefacts.” (Esther Keymolen, “The Focal Practice of 3D Printing,” in 3D Printing, Information Technology and Law Series 26 (2016))

Pictured: Spare parts for a variety of 3d printers. Thingiverse.

Travelling Lightly: 3D Printing in Space

Instead of carrying numerous, bulky spare parts into space, future missions may carry 3d printer + digital models and print out parts as needed. Currently being trialed by NASA (Sarah Loff, “International Space Station's 3-D Printer,” NASA: 3D Printing, 26 November 2014).

Image: NASA/Emmett Given

Rapid Prototyping

3D printing allows designers to test digital designs iteratively, by quickly instantiating a physical object after each design change.

Robohand (right) was built through long-distance collaboration between Richard As (engineer, South Africa) and Ivan Owen (special effects artist and puppeteer in Bellingham, Washington). They used 3d printing to iteratively revise their digital design and test prototypes.

Image: Robohand, “3D printed and aluminum CNC machined, anatomically driven, custom fitted, mechanical devices to help limb different individuals as an alternative to standard prosthetics”—a prosthetic hand with superior mechanical function, custom-fitted through 3d printing and responsive to wrist or back-of-hand movements. (Steve Henn and Cindy Carpien, “3-D Printer Brings Dexterity To Children With No Fingers,” Health News from NPR, 18 June 2013).

Medical Prosthetics

Matt Ratto, University of Toronto, Critical Making Lab: 3d printing artificial limbs and prostheses for the developing world.

3d scanning and printing technologies produce prostheses fitted to individual patients, with less demand on trained medical technicians' and patients' time, than current prosthesis-making practices.

“A relatively inexpensive 3D printer is connected to a laptop and the [local] prosthetics specialist scans the patient's stump. Special software analyzes the scan and a nylon socket is generated by the printer, to be attached to other parts needed for the full prosthetic, purchased from the Red Cross.” (May Warren, “University of Toronto developed solution for 3D printing artificial limbs going global,” The Toronto Star, 22 June 2016).

Bioprinting

  • Printing drug dosage (very precise, customizable delivery)
  • Printing biological tissues for medical applications (from drug trials to prostheses and “spare parts,” e.g. skin and cartilage) (Ashkan Shafiee and Anthony Atala, “Printing Technologies for Medical Applications,” Trends in Molecular Medicine 22.3 (March 2016): 254-265.

Bioprinting

  • 3d printed "bionic ear” interweaves cartilage tissue with electronics, “within the highly complex topology of a human ear.”
  • Tech: “An ordinary 3-D printer combines a matrix of hydrogel and calf cells with silver nanoparticles that form an antenna. The calf cells later developed into cartilage.”

Art

  • 3D printing enhances existing art:
  • In stop-motion animation, animators sculpt puppets, pose them, and capture one image at a time. For each facial expression of a character, animators have to sculpt another puppet (or another puppet head). In The Nightmare before Christmas,
  • 3D printing enables tweakable digital models + rapid fabrication
  • Animators can create a far wider variety of facial expressions, enhancing the subtlety of the puppets' “performance” (Peter Debruge, "Building a better puppet: Laika's 3D printing enhances stop-motion animation," Variety 13 Aug. 2012)
  • Before 3D printing: The Nightmare before Christmas' Jack has a few hundred expressions. Coraline's eponymous heroine has nearly 200,000. Paranorman's has 1.5 million.

Access to Artifacts

  • 3D printing permits access to fragile sites and artifacts, by allowing these to be scanned, digitally modelled, and then replicated cheaply and with reasonable accuracy
  • Examining fragile artifacts, researchers “can actually ‘lift' things out of the ground without really lifting them out of the ground. [They] do this by photographing the findings many times, weaving those photos together for a complete 3-D model, and then printing it.” (Katie McNally, “Preserving the Past in 3-D,” UVANews, 25 January 2016.)
  • 3D-printed replicas can be used in the classroom

Access to Artifacts II

Canadian Museum of Human Rights:

“photographs by artists with various degrees of vision loss”—given a tactile dimension through 3d modelling technologies.

How it works:

  • Original 2d image converted to 3d digital model
  • 3d digital model is 3d-printed, creating a relief
  • Original 2d image is accurately superimposed onto the digital model
  • Touch-activated sensors are embedded in the prints to provide audio descriptions and narration

Access to Sites

  • 3D modelling and printing helps researchers digitize fragile spaces
  • Case study: the highly fragile rupestrian churches of St. Eustache, the Meryemana and St. Daniel, in Kapadokya, Turkey (churches cut into the rock, with murals)
  • Using digital scanning and modelling, 3D printing, and augmented reality, researchers replicate these fragile spaces for public access (Giorgio Verdiani, “Bringing Impossible Places to the Public: Three Ideas for Rupestrian Churches in Goreme, Kapadokya Utilizing a Digital Survey, 3D Printing, and Augmented Reality,” Open Archaeology 2015:1: 131–143).

Restoration

3D printing, with other technologies (Micro–Computed Tomography, CAD), allows researchers to reconstruct incomplete artifacts (Laycock et al, “Using a Combination of Micro–Computed Tomography, CAD and 3D Printing Techniques to Reconstruct Incomplete 19th-Century Cantonese Chess Pieces,” Journal on Computing and Cultural Heritage, 7.4 (2015)).

Writing Exercise 2: Making as salvation

Focal things and practices disclose the world about us—our time, our place, our heritage, our hopes—and centre our lives. They lead us to say: ‘there is no place I would rather be. There is nothing I would rather do. There is no one I would rather be with.'

mass-produced artefacts do not invite users to interact with them in an engaging manner. In general, they are scripted 35 to be used as commodities rather than as focal things, which human beings actively interact with. Taking into account their often-concealed interior and low life expectancy, they are designed to fulfil a certain purpose and to be replaced when needed. Therefore, it is rather despite than due to this script they become part of an engaging technological practice…

To qualify as a focal thing, a 3D printer must not just produce a commodity, but also enable a more profound and engaging interaction with its users. It has to be ‘open’ or ‘transparent’ in order to let users interact with it in an active way. The user should be an essential part of its functioning. Moreover, as a focal thing, a 3D printer must function as an enabler of traditions and rituals, opening up new per- spectives on the world it mediate.

“Critical making invites reflection on the relationship of the maker to the thing produced, reflection on how elements (whether nuts and bolts, bits, and bytes, or breath, blood, flesh, brain, and neurons) work together— in short, consideration and awareness of the mediated and direct experiences of interacting with the material world.” (Matt Ratto, Megan Boler, and Ronald Deibert. Introduction. DIY Citizenship : Critical Making and Social Media. 2014)
